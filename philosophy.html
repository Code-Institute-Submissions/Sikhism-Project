The philosophy behind Sikhism

A look into the core foundations of Sikhism and a deeper understanding of the primary beliefs and principles.
Guru Nanak Dev Ji when founding Sikhism instilled three core principles every Sikh should adhere to.

Naam Japna: Remembrance and meditation of God

Photo: Sean Robertson/Unsplash

The concept of Naam Japna is very simple, by constantly thinking about God’s virtues such as love and kindness Sikhs are able to better develop a feeling of love and kindness for humanity. Being able to see the presence of God in everyone allows the Sikh to better appreciate others and helps to prevent the thought of hurting others. Naam is given the highest priority in the Sikh faith.

"With my hands I do God's work; with my tongue I sing God's glorious praises. With my feet, I walk on the path of my Lord and Master." ((1))

Guru Granth Sahib Ji, 189

Vand Chakna: Sharing wealth with those who are in need

Photo: Gian Carlo Delsante/Pixabay

Just as Sikhs remember God; they will also realize they are apart of a human brotherhood. This realization would naturally encourage Sikhs to help and share their wealth with those who are less fortunate. The sharing must be done without any motive of gain but as a sense of responsibility.

“They give and give, but ask a thousand-fold more, and hope that the world will honor them."

Guru Granth Sahib Ji, 446

Kirat Karna: Honest and truthful labour

Photo: Akshat Vats/Unsplash

Sikhs are expected to be active, to be doing rather than being idle. Sikhs should earn their living by honest means, without exploiting others for their expense. The body is meant to be up and working for the well-being of the family and the rest of society, but the mind should be tuned towards God. Sikhism is considered a way of life,

"Those who have meditated on the Naam, the Name of the Lord, and departed after having worked by the sweat of their brows - O Nanak, their faces are radiant in the Court of the Lord, and many are saved along with them. ||1||"

Guru Granth Sahib Ji, 8
